Pair Trading and Options: Practical Insights in Quantitative Finance

Published on April 7, 2025

Understanding Options and Futures

  • Options: Derivative contracts that give the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset at a set price within a specific period. The buyer pays a premium for this right.
    Buying a stock is related to a call option; selling a stock is related to a put option.
  • Futures: Contracts that obligate both the buyer and seller to transact an asset at a predetermined price on a future date. Both parties are bound to the contract.

Key Differences

  • Obligation vs. Right: Options give the buyer a right; futures create an obligation for both parties.
  • Settlement: Options are usually cash-settled; futures often involve physical settlement.
  • Execution: Options can be exercised anytime before expiry; futures only on the specified date.
  • Profit/Loss: Options limit loss to the premium; futures expose both parties to unlimited profit/loss.

Options as Hedging Tools

  • Covered Call (Short-Call Hedge): Selling call options on owned stock to limit downside risk and earn premiums.
    If the stock price doesn’t rise significantly, the trader keeps the premium.
  • Long Put Hedge: Buying put options to protect against price declines. If the stock price falls below the strike price, the option’s premium increases and offsets equity losses.

Decoding Option Greeks

  • Delta: Measures how much an option’s price changes with a 1-point move in the underlying asset.
    Delta for calls: 0 to 1; puts: 0 to -1. Indicates moneyness and hedge ratios.
  • Gamma: Rate of change of Delta. High for at-the-money options, low for in/out-of-the-money.
    Delta is the first derivative of premium; Gamma is the second.
  • Theta: Time decay; how much value an option loses as expiration approaches.
    Hurts buyers, benefits sellers. Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value (Theta affects time value).
  • Vega: Sensitivity to volatility; higher volatility increases option value.
    Vega = Rate of change of premium / Change in Volatility.
  • Rho: Sensitivity to interest rates. Positive for calls, negative for puts.
    Rho is less significant for most practical trading decisions.

Pair Trading: Mean Reversion and Statistical Arbitrage

Stock Selection and Rationale

  • Chosen Pair: Apple (AAPL) and Microsoft (MSFT)
    • Combined market cap of $5.51 trillion (April 2025), representing 12% of the S&P 500.
    • Apple dominates in premium consumer hardware and ecosystem integration; Microsoft leads in enterprise cloud and AI.
    • Both are financially robust but have different innovation strategies (Microsoft invests more in R&D; Apple focuses on vertical integration).
  • Sectoral Trends (2019–2025):
    • Cloud computing and AI adoption (Microsoft’s Azure, OpenAI partnerships).
    • Hardware innovation and ecosystem lock-in (Apple’s “Apple Intelligence”).
  • Correlation Analysis: 3-month rolling correlation of 0.53 (2019–2025), indicating moderate positive co-movement.
  • Cointegration: Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) statistic of -3.42 (p < 0.05), confirming a mean-reverting spread which is ideal for pair trading.

Data Collection and Preprocessing

  • Historical Data: Daily adjusted close prices for AAPL and MSFT (Jan 1, 2019 – Dec 31, 2023) from Yahoo Finance.
  • Handling Missing Values: Linear interpolation fills gaps due to market holidays, ensuring a continuous timeline for analysis.
  • Normalization: Min-max scaling brings both stocks to a 0–1 range, allowing fair comparison and visualization.
    Normalized price = (Actual Price – Minimum Price) / (Maximum Price – Minimum Price)
  • Visualization: Line charts of normalized prices and histograms of daily returns reveal trends and volatility.

Backtesting the Pair Trading Strategy

  1. Strategy Logic: Bet that two stocks that usually move together will return to their "normal" relationship if they temporarily drift apart.
    • If Apple rises sharply while Microsoft stays flat, sell Apple and buy Microsoft (and vice versa).
  2. Spread Calculation: Adjust for price differences using a hedge ratio (e.g., if MSFT moves ₹2 for every ₹1 AAPL moves, hedge ratio is 2).
  3. Trading Signals: Use Z-Score of the spread:
    • Z > +1.5: Sell Apple, Buy Microsoft.
    • Z < -1.5: Buy Apple, Sell Microsoft.
    • Exit when Z approaches zero.
  4. Performance Metrics:
    • Returns: Example: 20.83% over five years (₹12,000 to ₹14,500).
    • Sharpe Ratio: Measures risk-adjusted return (e.g., 0.5 means ₹0.50 earned per ₹1 of risk).
    • Max Drawdown: Largest peak-to-trough loss (e.g., -50% if ₹10,000 drops to ₹5,000).

Performance Analysis and Improvements

  • Robustness: The strategy exploits mean-reverting behavior, profiting when the price relationship normalizes.
  • Improvements:
    • Use machine learning models for missing value imputation (data cleaning).
    • Apply advanced normalization techniques (e.g., normal distribution) for better graphs and interpretation.
    • Adapt Z-score bands to volatility using machine learning for dynamic signal generation.

This structured approach, combining statistical rigor and practical trading logic, forms the backbone of robust quantitative finance strategies.
